Learning Objectives

By the end of this lecture, you should be able to:

  1. Describe the basic structure and mechanism of action of penicillins
  2. Explain the four mechanisms of bacterial resistance to β-lactams
  3. Classify β-lactamases by Ambler molecular class
  4. Differentiate the five classes of penicillins by spectrum
  5. Select appropriate penicillin therapy based on infection type
  6. Recognize the role of β-lactamase inhibitors in clinical practice

This lecture covers approximately 1 hour of material. The learning objectives span from basic science through clinical application. Emphasize that penicillins remain cornerstone antibiotics despite resistance challenges.

Historical Context

  • 1928: Alexander Fleming discovers penicillin from Penicillium notatum
  • 1940s: Florey & Chain enable commercial production
  • Mid-1940s: Penicillin G available in UK and US
  • Initiated the modern antibiotic era

Fleming’s discovery was serendipitous—he noticed bacterial growth inhibition around a contaminating mold. The critical advance was Florey and Chain’s work to purify and mass-produce penicillin. This earned them the 1945 Nobel Prize.

Penicillin Structure

Structure of penicillin showing the thiazolidine ring (1) and β-lactam ring (2).

The core structure consists of thiazolidine ring, β-lactam ring, and side chain (R group). The β-lactam ring is essential for activity. The side chain determines spectrum and pharmacokinetics.

Key Structural Elements

  • Thiazolidine ring — 5-membered ring containing sulfur
  • β-Lactam ring — 4-membered ring essential for activity
  • Side chain (R group) — Determines:
    • Antibacterial spectrum
    • Acid stability (oral absorption)
    • β-Lactamase resistance
    • Pharmacokinetic properties

The side chain modifications differentiate the various penicillin classes. Adding a bulky side chain creates steric hindrance protecting against β-lactamases. Adding an amino group expands gram-negative coverage.

Mechanism of Action

Important

Key Concept: Penicillins inhibit bacterial cell wall synthesis by binding to penicillin-binding proteins (PBPs)

  • Target: Peptidoglycan synthesis — final cross-linking step
  • Effect: Weakened cell wall → osmotic lysis
  • Bactericidal (but not against non-growing cells)

The cell wall is essential for bacterial survival. Peptidoglycan is the main structural component. Penicillins are bactericidal because cell lysis is irreversible. Cells not actively growing are tolerant.

Peptidoglycan Structure

PBP transpeptidation reaction cross-linking bacterial cell wall.

Peptidoglycan is composed of long chains of alternating NAG-NAM disaccharides. Cross-links between pentapeptides give the cell wall strength. In gram-positives, the layer is thick (50-100 molecules). In gram-negatives, it’s thin but protected by the outer membrane.

Penicillin-Binding Proteins (PBPs)

PBP Function Effect of Inhibition
PBP1 Transpeptidase Cell lysis
PBP2 Cell elongation Round cells, lysis
PBP3 Cell division Filamentous cells
  • PBPs are serine proteases (related to β-lactamases!)
  • Different β-lactams have different PBP affinities

β-lactamases evolved from PBPs—they share structural features. PBPs bind penicillin tightly (slow deacylation = inhibition), while β-lactamases rapidly hydrolyze penicillin (fast deacylation = resistance).

Four Mechanisms of Resistance

  1. β-Lactamase production — enzymatic destruction (most common)
  2. Decreased permeability — porin mutations in gram-negatives
  3. Efflux pumps — active drug removal
  4. Altered PBPs — low-affinity binding (e.g., MRSA’s PBP2a)

Warning

Multiple mechanisms often coexist, especially in Pseudomonas aeruginosa

β-Lactamase production is by far the most common. In gram-negatives, β-lactamases intercept drugs in the periplasmic space. MRSA produces PBP2a with very low affinity for most β-lactams.

Ambler Classification of β-Lactamases

Class Active Site Representative Key Substrates
A Serine TEM, KPC, CTX-M Penicillins, cephalosporins
B Metallo (Zn) NDM, VIM, IMP All except aztreonam
C Serine AmpC, CMY Cephalosporins
D Serine OXA Oxacillin, carbapenems

This classification is based on amino acid sequence. Classes A, C, D are serine β-lactamases. Class B uses zinc ions. No approved inhibitor works against class B—this is a major unmet need.

Classification of Penicillins

  1. Natural penicillins: Penicillin G, Penicillin V
  2. Penicillinase-resistant: Nafcillin, Oxacillin, Dicloxacillin
  3. Aminopenicillins: Ampicillin, Amoxicillin
  4. Carboxypenicillins: Ticarcillin (obsolete)
  5. Ureidopenicillins: Piperacillin (used with tazobactam)

Each class represents modifications to the side chain. Natural penicillins have narrowest spectrum but highest activity. Penicillinase-resistant have bulky side chains. Aminopenicillins add gram-negative coverage.

Natural Penicillins: Spectrum

Most Active Against:

  • Streptococcus spp. (including S. pneumoniae, S. pyogenes)
  • Neisseria meningitidis
  • Treponema pallidum (syphilis)
  • Anaerobes (except Bacteroides fragilis)
  • Listeria monocytogenes

Natural penicillins remain drugs of choice for several infections. Penicillin G remains first-line for syphilis. For streptococcal infections, resistance is rare.

Penicillinase-Resistant Penicillins

Spectrum: MSSA only

Drug Route Key Features
Nafcillin IV Hepatic metabolism; hypokalemia
Oxacillin IV Hepatotoxicity risk
Dicloxacillin PO 96% protein binding

Warning

NOT active against MRSA — MRSA has altered PBP2a

These agents resist staphylococcal β-lactamases due to bulky side chains. They are ineffective against MRSA. For serious MSSA infections, nafcillin or oxacillin are preferred over vancomycin.

Aminopenicillins

Aminopenicillins: Ampicillin and Amoxicillin structures.

The key structural feature is the amino group. This increases gram-negative activity. Amoxicillin has better oral absorption (74-80% vs 33-54%). Both are susceptible to β-lactamases.

Aminopenicillins: Clinical Use

Ampicillin (IV preferred):

  • Meningitis: Listeria, Group B strep
  • Enterococcal infections (E. faecalis)

Amoxicillin (oral):

  • Otitis media (high-dose for resistant pneumococci)
  • Community-acquired pneumonia
  • H. pylori eradication

High-dose amoxicillin (80-90 mg/kg/day in children) overcomes penicillin-resistant pneumococci. For adult pneumonia, standard-dose amoxicillin is recommended for outpatients without comorbidities.

Piperacillin-Tazobactam

  • Piperacillin: Broadest spectrum penicillin
    • Pseudomonas aeruginosa coverage
    • Excellent anaerobic coverage
  • Tazobactam: β-lactamase inhibitor
    • Restores activity against class A enzymes
    • Does NOT inhibit AmpC or metallo-enzymes

Tip

Clinical Pearl: Extended infusions (over 4 hours) maximize time above MIC

Piperacillin-tazobactam is one of the most commonly used broad-spectrum antibiotics. Extended infusion dosing has become standard practice because β-lactam efficacy depends on time above MIC.

Traditional inhibitors work as “suicide inhibitors.” Newer agents have broader activity. Note that NO approved inhibitor works against class B metallo-β-lactamases.

Pharmacokinetics: Key Points

  • Oral absorption varies:
    • Ampicillin: 30-55% (decreased by food)
    • Amoxicillin: 74-92% (unaffected by food)
  • Protein binding: 17% to 97%
  • Elimination: Primarily renal (except nafcillin)
  • Half-life: Short (0.5-1.5 hours)

Short half-lives necessitate frequent dosing (every 4-6 hours). Renal dosing adjustments are needed for most penicillins except nafcillin and antistaphylococcal agents.

Renal Dosing

Penicillin G 75% dose Dose post-HD

Piperacillin 3g q8-12h 3g q12h

NC = No change

Antistaphylococcal penicillins don’t require renal adjustment (hepatic elimination). For hemodialysis patients, dose after dialysis.

Hypersensitivity Reactions

  • Most important adverse effect
  • Range: Rash → Anaphylaxis
  • True anaphylaxis is rare (<0.01%)
  • History of “penicillin allergy” often inaccurate
    • Only ~2% will react if challenged

Tip

Clinical Pearl: Skin testing can safely de-label most patients

Penicillin allergy is over-reported. Many patients labeled allergic never had true allergy or have lost sensitivity. De-labeling programs are safe and effective.

Clinical Case 1

55-year-old man with cellulitis

  • No systemic symptoms
  • No risk factors for MRSA
  • No drug allergies

Question: What is the optimal oral therapy?

Answer: Dicloxacillin or cephalexin (targeting MSSA and streptococci)

Without MRSA risk factors, target MSSA and streptococci. Dicloxacillin is narrow-spectrum and highly effective.

Clinical Case 2

68-year-old woman with pneumonia

  • Community-acquired, outpatient
  • COPD, no recent antibiotics
  • No drug allergies

Question: Appropriate empiric therapy?

Answer: Amoxicillin 1g TID (or amoxicillin-clavulanate if concern for H. influenzae)

High-dose amoxicillin covers S. pneumoniae including strains with reduced susceptibility. If COPD (H. influenzae risk), amoxicillin-clavulanate preferred.

Clinical Case 3

45-year-old with intra-abdominal infection

  • Post-operative abscess
  • Recent hospitalization

Question: What empiric therapy covers gram-negatives, anaerobes, and resistance?

Answer: Piperacillin-tazobactam 4.5g q6h extended infusion

Piperacillin-tazobactam covers Enterobacterales, Pseudomonas, and anaerobes. Extended infusion optimizes pharmacodynamics. If ESBL risk factors, consider carbapenem.

Summary: Key Takeaways

  1. β-Lactam ring is essential; its hydrolysis = resistance
  2. β-Lactamases are the most common resistance mechanism
  3. Classification: Natural → Antistaphylococcal → Amino → Antipseudomonal
  4. Match the spectrum to the pathogen
  5. β-Lactamase inhibitors extend coverage but have limitations
  6. Most “penicillin allergies” can be safely de-labeled

These are the core concepts. Emphasize the relationship between structure and function. The principle of matching spectrum to pathogen is fundamental to antibiotic stewardship.
